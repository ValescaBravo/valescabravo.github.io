Bellabeat — Product Analytics Case Study (R)

Product-style insights from wearable activity data, translated into engagement hypotheses and measurement-ready recommendations.

R EDA Product Analytics Storytelling

Executive summary

  • What it is: a product analytics case study using public wearable activity logs to explore behavioural patterns and inform engagement strategy proposals.
  • Dataset type: aggregated activity and wellness signals (e.g., steps, activity intensity, calories and sleep logs) captured by fitness trackers.
  • Approach: data cleaning, exploratory analysis and behavioural slicing (time-of-day, day-of-week, activity mix), then translating patterns into product/marketing hypotheses.
  • Output: a narrative report (EDA + charts) and a set of recommendations mapped to observed patterns, each paired with a measurement idea.
  • Tooling: R / RStudio (tidy data workflows and visual exploration).

Business context & goal

Bellabeat is a wellness brand operating in the wearables and health-app category, where sustained engagement (not just initial downloads) is a key driver of long-term value. In this case study, I treat the dataset as a proxy for how people may use consumer fitness tracking features.

Goal: understand usage patterns in wearable activity data and propose engagement strategies that could be tested in a real product context.

Stakeholders & questions

Who would use these insights?
Product manager Which behaviours indicate habit formation vs drop-off? Supports feature prioritisation (nudges, goals, summaries) and retention experiments.
Marketing lead When and how should we message users? Aligns lifecycle comms with real usage rhythms (timing, content themes, segmentation).
Leadership What engagement bets are worth testing? Turns descriptive patterns into a focused test roadmap and measurable outcomes.

Data & method

The dataset represents wearable activity logs recorded over time. It is suitable for descriptive analysis of usage behaviour: time-based trends, activity mix, and variability across users.

Privacy: this page does not display personal fields or raw user identifiers. Any analysis is discussed at an aggregated, behavioural level.
  • Cleaning: standardised timestamps, resolved duplicates/missing entries, and aligned daily vs hourly granularity.
  • Exploration: distributions (activity levels, sleep duration), time patterns (hour/day/week), and variability across users.
  • Behavioural slicing: compared patterns by day-of-week, time-of-day, and activity intensity mix.
  • Storytelling: converted findings into product hypotheses and proposed measurement plans.
  • Tools: R / RStudio.

Key insights (observed patterns)

1) Activity levels show high variability

The dataset indicates wide differences in daily movement between users and across days, suggesting distinct engagement “profiles” (consistent trackers vs sporadic users).

Interpretation: segmentation is likely more useful than one-size-fits-all messaging.

2) Usage rhythms cluster around daily routines

Patterns suggest activity tends to concentrate around routine windows (commutes, breaks, evenings), rather than being evenly distributed.

Interpretation: timing and context can improve the relevance of prompts and content.

3) Sedentary time is a dominant baseline

A large portion of recorded time is low-activity, with bursts of moderate/vigorous movement. This indicates opportunity for “small wins” that fit real schedules.

Interpretation: micro-interventions may be more realistic than large behaviour changes.

4) Day-of-week patterns suggest changing intent

The dataset suggests behaviour differs across weekdays vs weekends (routine-led days vs flexible days), which can inform content themes and nudges.

Interpretation: adapt messaging to “structured” vs “flex” days.

5) Sleep signals are inconsistent across users

Sleep logging and duration appear uneven across participants, indicating that not all users track or maintain sleep habits consistently.

Interpretation: sleep features may benefit from onboarding and clear value framing.

6) Tracking completeness varies

Some users contribute richer logs than others, suggesting that “data availability” itself may be an engagement indicator (and a constraint).

Interpretation: design journeys that work even with partial tracking.

Recommendations (proposed actions)

Segment users by engagement profile

Proposed action: define lightweight segments (consistent, intermittent, onboarding) using recent tracking activity and completeness.

Intended outcome: more relevant nudges and content, reduced message fatigue.

Measurement idea: WAU/DAU proxy, tracking-days per week, notification opt-out rate.

Time nudges to routine windows

Proposed action: schedule prompts around observed routine clusters (e.g., midday check-in, evening recap) with user-controlled preferences.

Intended outcome: improve prompt relevance and repeat usage.

Measurement idea: prompt open rate, same-day activity logging after prompt, 7-day retention proxy.

Design “small win” movement interventions

Proposed action: introduce short, low-friction movement goals (2–5 minute breaks, streaks, gentle challenges).

Intended outcome: reduce sedentary baseline and build habit loops.

Measurement idea: sedentary-minutes share (proxy), completion rate of micro-goals, streak continuation.

Weekday vs weekend messaging strategy

Proposed action: tailor content themes: “routine support” on weekdays; “longer activities / reflection” on weekends.

Intended outcome: match user intent and increase engagement with insights.

Measurement idea: content click-through by day-of-week, session frequency split weekday/weekend.

Onboard sleep tracking with value cues

Proposed action: add guided setup, reminders, and a simple “sleep story” (consistency over perfection).

Intended outcome: increase adoption of sleep features and improve data completeness.

Measurement idea: sleep-feature adoption rate, weekly sleep-log days, feature return rate.

Build around partial data

Proposed action: ensure insights and summaries degrade gracefully (e.g., “based on the days you tracked…”), and encourage incremental tracking.

Intended outcome: keep users engaged even when logs are incomplete.

Measurement idea: % users receiving personalised summaries, completion of “add one more day” prompts.

Limitations

  • Public dataset: not collected from Bellabeat’s product and may not reflect Bellabeat customer behaviour.
  • Sample not necessarily representative; participation and tracking completeness may bias observed patterns.
  • No causal attribution: patterns are descriptive and cannot prove that a change will cause an outcome.
  • No access to product instrumentation (events, funnels, cohorts), so engagement metrics here are proposed rather than measured.

Next steps (with real product data)

  • Instrumentation: define an event taxonomy (onboarding, goal setting, notification interactions, insights views).
  • Cohorts: retention by acquisition channel, device type, and early engagement signals (first 7 days).
  • Funnels: activation funnel (install → permission → first track → first insight → repeat week).
  • Experimentation: A/B test prompt timing, messaging, and “small win” interventions with guardrails.
  • Outcome metrics: WAU/retention, feature adoption, habit proxies (tracked days/week), and churn predictors.
